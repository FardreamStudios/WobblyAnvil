# Wobbly Anvil — Worldbuilding & Character Jam

## Purpose of this doc

Companion to `AdventureModeJam.md`. Where that doc defined the *structure* of Adventure Mode (reigns, loadouts, decree rewards, engagement loops), this doc defines the *soul* — who the smith is, who's around them, and what makes their craft specifically theirs.

No code. No spec. Pure design. Written as a jam transcript so the reasoning survives alongside the decisions, because future sessions will want to know *why* things landed where they did, not just *what* was chosen.

This doc covers **Block 1: The Smith**. Blocks 2 (forging as a verb), 3 (components), and 4 (meta vs. run sort) are still open and flagged at the bottom.

---

## The Framing Problem We Were Solving

The Adventure Mode doc jumped ahead to structural questions without first answering the foundational one: **what is a smith, and what does forging mean as a verb in this game?** Without that, every downstream decision — upgrades, techniques, vocabulary, loops — was floating. Block 1 is about nailing the character so everything else has a foundation to build on.

---

## The Smith — Locked Decisions

### Identity: Prodigy on Trial

The smith is a **prodigy** — young, gifted, untested. Not a master, not a journeyman, not a disgraced veteran. Someone who shouldn't be at the king's court yet but is, because someone saw something in them.

**Why prodigy and not the alternatives:**

- *Master* had nowhere to grow. The arc we wanted (smith grows into warrior through the weapon) requires a starting point with room to climb. A master has already climbed.
- *Journeyman* was grounded and tempting, but emotionally flat. A prodigy carries natural tension — "they picked me, and I don't fully know why" — that a journeyman's "prove I belong" can't match.
- *Disgraced* was the default indie protagonist. Locked the character into one emotional register (guilt/shame). Overused. Also competed with the king for the "judgment" role.

**The key constraint:** the prodigy is *only* a prodigy at the forge. They are not secretly a warrior, not a prophesied hero, not a hidden bloodline. Their gift is narrow and specific — they are extraordinarily good at craft, and ordinary at everything else. This is essential because the weapon has to be the protagonist. If the smith is also special, the weapon gets crowded out.

### Why The King Trusts A Prodigy — The Audition

The obvious problem with "prodigy" is why the king would trust someone untested with real decrees. The answer solves it elegantly: **the king doesn't trust you yet.** The early decrees are the audition.

This reframes the entire opening of a reign:

- Early decrees are narratively smaller because the king is testing you, not because the game needs a tutorial
- As you succeed, decrees escalate because you've earned harder ones
- Failure doesn't just mean "you lost" — it means "you confirmed his doubts"
- The king becomes a *character*, not a quest-giver. He's watching. Every decree is him deciding whether to keep investing in you
- Different kings per reign can have different tempers — patient, cruel, desperate. This gives free roguelite variety without new systems

**Bonus:** this also answers the open question in the Adventure Mode doc about early-game ramp ("how do first runs feel meaningful before the loadout pool is deep?"). The answer: because the early decrees are *supposed* to be small. Audition phase. That's a feature, not a scaling problem.

### Growth Arc: The Weapon Makes The Warrior

The smith starts as a craftsman forced into the field — fragile, weapon-dependent, not a fighter. But over the course of a reign, they grow into a warrior. **Not because they trained to be one. Because the weapon they built taught them how to fight.**

The arc shape:

- **Early reign** — fragile, terrified, every adventure is survival
- **Mid reign** — the weapon is growing, you're learning its language, you're becoming dangerous *through* it
- **Late reign** — you and the weapon are one thing

This flip matters because it reframes the smith's progression entirely. The smith barely changes as a character sheet. **The weapon is the progression.** That's unusual and valuable — it keeps the forge central forever, it makes Legendary Actions meaningful (they're milestones of the weapon teaching you), and it gives death a specific flavor: you don't die because you were weak, you die because the weapon wasn't ready, or because the king's trust outgrew what the weapon could carry.

If the weapon breaks late-reign, you don't just lose a tool — **you lose the thing that made you capable**. You revert to the fragile craftsman you started as. That's devastating in a way "I lost my sword" never could be.

### Signature Verb: Combine + Break-and-Rebuild

This is the gameplay hook that defines what this specific prodigy does that no other smith does.

**Primary verb: Combine.** The prodigy puts together things that shouldn't go together. Other smiths follow recipes. This prodigy experiments with synthesis — iron with bone, moonsilver with fire-resin, salt with edge-work — and somehow it works.

**Secondary verb: Break and rebuild.** When a combine doesn't work, the prodigy can shatter their half-finished weapon and start over with the wreckage. Not a clean refund — they lose materials, they lose time, but they *keep the lesson*. The broken weapon teaches them what doesn't work, and the next attempt is smarter for it.

**Why this pairing is the right one:**

- Combine is **synthesis**, which is a creative act. Recipe-following is a lookup table. Combine makes the forge a place where the player *wonders what happens if*, which is the headspace of every great roguelite.
- Combine solves the Adventure Mode doc's "forging vocabulary" problem in one stroke. The vocabulary isn't a list of recipes — it's a list of ingredients and their interactions. Players learn that dragonbone reacts with moonsilver, that salt poisons fire magic, that bone marrow holds enchantments longer than steel does. That's a *language*, not a manual. Same shape as Slay the Spire's Exhaust/Block/Strength.
- Break-and-rebuild is the recovery mechanic for bold experimentation. Without it, aggressive combining is too punishing. With it, the prodigy is the only smith reckless enough to try the weird stuff, because they're the only one willing to destroy their own work to learn from it.
- Break-and-rebuild is a **personality trait expressed as a game mechanic**. Other smiths value their work too much. The prodigy is young/hungry/reckless enough to shatter a half-finished weapon. That's characterization through system.
- The pairing is a **fractal of the roguelite loop itself.** Each forge session is a small reign — you experiment, you fail, you break it down, you learn, you try again. Each reign is a long forge session. Beautiful symmetry.

**What this unlocks for late-reign weapons:**

The verb scales from tutorial to endgame without ever being replaced. What changes is the *ingredients*:

- **Early reign:** Simple combines. Iron + oak. Steel + leather. Looks like normal smithing.
- **Mid reign:** Strange materials from decree rewards. Bone with steel, moonsilver with fire-resin, poison with edge-work. Some combinations fail. You break and rebuild. You learn.
- **Late reign:** You're doing **alchemical smithing** — rendering materials, combining essences, pushing past what metal can hold. The weapon is a *working*, not a sword. Something an ordinary smith would not recognize as a weapon at all.

The action your hands are doing never changes. The world around the action gets stranger. That's the sign of a well-chosen verb.

## The Fairy — Who She Really Is

The single biggest move in this jam. The fairy is the NPC who makes the whole worldbuilding land.

### The Locked Picture

- **She is secretly Hephaestus.** She never says this. The player may go the entire game without learning it explicitly.
- **She does not look like a fairy.** She is short, grubby, and mean-tempered. She reads as a rough little gremlin of a woman. "Fairy" is a mechanical/categorical label in this world, not a visual one. Why she's called a fairy is itself a quiet mystery — the answer being that "fairy" in this place, in this language, means something older and weirder than fantasy genre convention expects.
- **She has always been at the forge.** The prodigy cannot remember a time without her. She eats their food, uses their tools, complains about their work, and occasionally grunts a suggestion that turns out to be suspiciously good. The prodigy thinks of her the way you'd think of a feral cat that adopted you when you were small.
- **She's in the adventure party as mage/healer.** Permanent member. Never dies.
- **She arranged for the king to summon this specific prodigy.** Not through any grand gesture — through patient, invisible arrangement. A weapon falling into the right hands. A rumor reaching the right ear. The prodigy thinks they were discovered. They were *delivered*.
- **She has done this before.** This is not her first prodigy. It will not be her last.
- **When the prodigy dies, she grieves briefly and meanly, and starts again.**
- **None of this is ever stated in dialogue.** It sits underneath the character and lets the writing pull from it.

### Why Grubby-And-Mean Beats Mystical

The original pitch was a tiny glowing fairy on the workbench — the genre shorthand for "magical helper." The actual answer is much better because it hides the reveal at the character level rather than just the dialogue level.

- **A glowing fairy is a thin disguise.** Players are already primed to read "magical entity."
- **A grubby mean woman is camouflage.** Players spend forty hours thinking she's a weird sidekick before god-suspicion enters the frame.
- **Mean is a generous writing choice.** A kind mentor figure is easy and dull. A mean one is funny, specific, and has room to be tender *rarely*, which hits ten times harder when it happens.
- **She can swear at a god and nobody blinks.** Because she *is* one. The player reads it as grumpy companion being grumpy. That's the joke, playing forever.
- **The reveal is gameplay-hidden, not narrative-hidden.** Players will recontextualize a hundred past interactions when they figure it out — not because of a cutscene, but because they've been watching her react to their work the whole time.

### Why She's In The Party

This was the unexpected move that welded the game together. The doc had a structural problem it wasn't naming — the forge half and the adventure half were two different experiences with two different casts. The only thing crossing over was the prodigy. That's a weak seam.

Putting her in the party fixes it:

- **Continuous presence.** She's there at the forge. She's there in combat. She's there when the king speaks. She's the one constant in a cast that otherwise rotates.
- **The workshop isn't abandoned when you leave for adventure** — she comes with you, the fire stays lit.
- **Adventure mode gets a voice that knows the weapon,** because she watched you make it.
- **The party stops being a cast of strangers** — at least one party member is *yours*.
- **Between-reign continuity has a face.** When the prodigy dies, she's the witness to every reign. She's seen them all.
- **She fixes the loneliness problem without diluting the "alone with the craft" feeling,** because she's so grouchy she barely counts as company.

### Why Mage/Healer Is The Right Combat Slot

- **It's a support role.** Players read support characters as "not the star." Keeps her buried.
- **It justifies why she's in the party at all.** The fragile early-reign prodigy *needs* a healer. Of course she comes along. It's practical and the player accepts it immediately.
- **It hides her power in plain sight.** Mages do impossible things. When she heals a mortal wound or lights fire from nothing, it reads as "she's a mage," not "she's a god."
- **Hephaestus is a maker, not a spellcaster.** So her magic should feel scrappy, unorthodox — craft magic dressed up as healing. Not fireballs. Patching, hammering, mending.
- **Her heals should feel like repairs, not blessings.** Visual and audio language quietly echoing the forge. Players won't consciously notice. But when the reveal lands, they'll realize they've been watching Hephaestus repair them with smithing magic for the entire game.
- **Healers watch everything.** They stand in the back. They see every fight from outside it. She has a perfect vantage point to observe the prodigy growing across a reign. Healing is a side effect. *Watching* is her real job.

### The Fairy ↔ Signature Verb Connection

This is where everything fits together. The prodigy's signature verb — combine what shouldn't combine — **is Hephaestus's signature verb.** He was the god of unreasonable synthesis. He welded lightning into spears, breath into armor, sunlight into chains.

So when the fairy watches this prodigy combining things other smiths wouldn't touch, she isn't just watching a talented kid. She's watching someone who does *her verb*. That's why she chose this one. Not the best smith alive — the first smith in a long time who *combines the way she does*.

The prodigy's gift is genuine. But the reason she chose them, and the reason their work keeps coming out better than it should, is that they share her verb. She's patient with them for that reason. She grieves when they die for that reason.

And here's the long-tail reveal mechanism: as the game goes on, the fairy can comment on combinations in ways that are suspiciously specific. *"That'll hold. The salt wants to be near the iron." "Don't bother, those two hate each other."* Early on, players take it as grumpy flavor. Later, they notice she's always right about combines. Later still, they realize she's *only* right about combines and never about anything else. And then eventually: *oh.*

The twist emerges from the gameplay loop itself, because players have been watching her react to their signature verb for a hundred hours. No cutscene required.

## The Cast — Final Lineup

| Character | Role | Where They Live | Persists? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The Prodigy** | Player, self-taught, on trial | Forge + adventure | One reign |
| **The Fairy** | Permanent companion, mage/healer, secretly Hephaestus | Forge + adventure | **Every reign** |
| **The King** | Examiner, gives decrees, judges outcomes | Off-screen (decree-based) | One reign |
| **The Party** | Fixed-role adventurers, fill gaps your weapon doesn't | Adventure only | One reign (fairy is the exception) |

Small, legible, every character has one job. The fairy is the only non-prodigy character who persists across every reign. That's a unique narrative load she alone carries.

## The Forge — As A Place

- **It is the prodigy's forge.** Small, rough, self-built. Not the king's, not a mentor's. Their own.
- **The prodigy is self-taught.** Or believes they are. No prior human mentor. Never existed. From the prodigy's perspective, they figured out the craft alone — which is of course technically true, because the fairy is not a mentor in any formal sense. She's just been around, grumbling, for as long as they can remember.
- **The workshop is a private, almost sacred place.** Just the prodigy, the fire, and the fairy. No apprentice, no rival, no family. That fits the pressure fantasy. It also keeps the cast small.
- **The forge persists across reigns** (along with forge upgrades, per the Adventure Mode doc). But the reframe is worth noting: the upgrades aren't the prodigy's. **They're the fairy's, collected across many smiths over many generations.** She lets each new prodigy use them. This reframes the meta-progression as *her* patient work across time, not the player character's personal empire. It fits her role and keeps the prodigy humble.

## What This All Unlocks

Pulling back — here's what the worldbuilding now gives us that it didn't before:

- **A four-way emotional load on every forge session.** The prodigy works *for* the king, *under* the fairy's watch, *with* the weapon itself, and *toward* whatever decree is pending. Every hammer swing has four audiences. That density loads meaning into the room around the QTE rather than into the QTE itself — which is exactly how you make craft minigames feel weighty without having to engineer the weight into the minigame mechanics.

- **A specific failure flavor.** When the prodigy fails, it's not "they weren't strong enough." It's "the king was right to doubt me" plus "my craft wasn't good enough" plus (unspoken) "the fairy has to do this again with someone else." Three layers of shame, all traceable to craft.

- **A reveal that pays off gameplay, not cutscenes.** The fairy's hidden identity pays off *retroactively* across hundreds of hours of ordinary interactions. That's the most durable kind of twist — it can't be spoiled for anyone who hasn't logged the hours.

- **A meta-layer with a face.** Forge upgrades aren't abstract. They're *hers*. Every persistent thing in the game has a mythological owner, which makes meta-progression feel *personal* instead of systemic.

- **A growth arc that survives the roguelite structure.** The smith doesn't need permanent stat upgrades because the *weapon* is where growth lives. Each reign is a fresh weapon. Each weapon is a fresh arc. The smith stays a prodigy forever. The weapons are what change.

## Open Questions (Worldbuilding Only)

Things we touched on but didn't fully lock. These can be resolved in future sessions alongside Block 2/3/4 work.

1. **Does the king's personality change the fairy's behavior?** Different kings have different tempers. Does the fairy react differently to a cruel king vs. a patient one? She's seen them all — she should have opinions.
2. **Does the fairy have a name?** If so, is it a disguise name, or is it something she answers to casually that's actually a forgotten epithet of Hephaestus?
3. **How does the fairy describe herself if asked?** "I'm a fairy" is the obvious answer. Does she ever get more specific? Does she lie, deflect, or tell a partial truth?
4. **Does the prodigy age across reigns, or is each reign a different prodigy?** The Adventure Mode doc implies each reign is a fresh run — which means each reign is likely a different prodigy, and the fairy is outliving them one by one. Worth confirming because it changes the emotional math.
5. **What do other party members think of the fairy?** They rotate per reign. Do they treat her with deference, mockery, confusion? If some party members *recognize* her — even partially — that's a crack in the disguise.
6. **Is there anyone in the world who knows?** A rival smith, an old priest, a dying king who figured it out on his deathbed? A single character who *knows* would be a powerful late-game reveal mechanism.
7. **Why did she stop making her own things?** Hephaestus was a maker. Why is she watching mortals make things now instead of making them herself? There's a mythological answer buried here that could carry real weight if we find the right one.
